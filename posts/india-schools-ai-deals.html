India's Classrooms Are About to Get an AI Makeover — Canva and Google Are Leading It

By Yash June 20, 2026 5 min read

While the world's biggest tech companies fight over chips, compute and billion-dollar talent poaching, a quieter but arguably more important AI story is unfolding inside Indian classrooms. Two major state governments just signed AI-focused deals that could shape how millions of Indian students learn — and how lakhs of teachers teach.

Andhra Pradesh + Canva: Visual AI Comes to Government Schools

The Andhra Pradesh government has signed a deal with Canva to integrate visual AI communication tools into its government schools. The idea: give teachers and students access to AI-powered design tools that can turn lesson plans, presentations, and learning material into more engaging, visual formats — without needing any design background.

This builds on a pattern already seen elsewhere in India — Canva has previously partnered with CBSE to train over 840,000 teachers in visual communication and generative AI tools, reaching more than 25 million students nationwide. Andhra Pradesh's move brings that same kind of AI-powered visual learning directly into state government schools.

Maharashtra + Google: Training 4 Lakh+ Teachers, Free of Cost

The bigger headline, though, comes from Maharashtra. The state's School Education Department signed a Letter of Intent with Google for Education to train more than 4 lakh (400,000+) teachers across government and aided schools in AI literacy and digital skills — completely free of cost to the state.

💬 Maharashtra Education Minister Dadaji Bhuse: "The objective is to ensure that AI-based learning reaches every school in Maharashtra. The initiative will not impose any financial burden on the state government as Google will provide the training free of cost."

The 18-month programme — part of Google's global "AI Educator Series" — will be delivered in Marathi, Hindi, and English to ensure teachers across the state can actually use it, not just technically have access to it. It's designed to be mobile-first and practical, focused on real classroom use rather than abstract AI theory.

This Is Bigger Than Just Maharashtra

Google's AI Educator Series isn't limited to one state. It's already collaborating with the governments of Chhattisgarh, Assam, the Union Territory of Ladakh, and the Punjab School Education Board — with training localized into six Indian languages including Assamese, Telugu, Odia, and Punjabi. Maharashtra is the latest, and one of the largest, states to join this wave.

Combined with Andhra Pradesh's history of pushing AI curriculum — the state had earlier proposed introducing AI and tools like ChatGPT into school syllabi as part of broader education reforms — a clear national pattern is emerging: Indian state governments are racing to make AI literacy a basic part of public education, not a privilege reserved for private schools.

Why This Matters More Than It Sounds

It's easy to see headlines about billion-dollar AI compute deals and assume that's where the "real" AI story is. But teacher training at this scale is arguably more consequential for India's long-term AI competitiveness. A child in a government school in rural Maharashtra learning from a teacher trained on Gemini-based tools today could be building India's next AI startup in a decade.

This also connects directly to a trend Trendora covered recently — nearly 20% of India's AI learners now come from Tier-II and non-metro cities. Government-led teacher training programs like these are exactly the kind of infrastructure that turns that early interest into real, lasting capability.

What to Watch Next

The real test will be execution — whether 4 lakh teachers across Maharashtra's government schools genuinely get trained well, and whether the content stays relevant rather than becoming a one-time formality. If it works, expect more states to follow Maharashtra and Andhra Pradesh's lead fast.

🔥 Bottom line: While companies fight over GPUs and top AI researchers, India's states are quietly building something potentially more important — an AI-literate teaching workforce. That's the kind of foundation that compounds over a generation.
