AI Is Taking Over Jobs — And It's Already Happening

By Yash May 8, 2026 6 min read

For years, the conversation around AI and jobs went like this: "It might happen someday. Don't worry too much. New jobs will be created." That conversation is over. The numbers are in, the layoffs are real, and the companies aren't hiding the reason anymore.

Welcome to 2026 — where AI isn't a future threat. It's the current reality.

The Numbers Don't Lie

Let's start with the hardest fact. Goldman Sachs researchers found that AI is erasing roughly 16,000 net jobs every single month in the US alone — about 25,000 positions eliminated by AI substitution, with only 9,000 new roles created to offset it. And the pain is falling hardest on one group: Gen Z and entry-level workers.

These aren't hypothetical projections from a think tank. This is Goldman Sachs measuring what already happened over the past year.

AI substitution is wiping out roughly 25,000 jobs per month — while augmentation is only adding back about 9,000. The math doesn't work in workers' favour right now.

The Companies Saying It Out Loud

One of the most telling shifts in 2026 is that companies have stopped pretending. They're naming AI directly as the reason for cuts.

Ticketmaster recently cut 8% of its entire global workforce — around 350 employees across 25 countries. The official reason from their Global President? "AI as a new utility." Not market conditions. Not restructuring. AI.

PayPal has announced plans to eliminate around 20% of its workforce — close to 4,760 jobs — over the next two to three years, explicitly citing AI-driven efficiency as the driver.

Meta is cutting 8,000 jobs — roughly 10% of its total headcount — while simultaneously pouring record capital into AI infrastructure and large language models. Their Chief People Officer put it plainly in an internal memo: the goal is to "run the company more efficiently." Translation: fewer humans, more AI.

And in journalism, over 500 jobs were cut in just the first three months of 2026, on top of more than 3,400 journalism cuts across the US and UK throughout 2025. The Washington Post alone cut more than 300 of its roughly 800 journalists.

Which Jobs Are Being Hit First?

Not all jobs are equally exposed. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report has identified the roles declining fastest between 2025 and 2030: bank tellers, data entry clerks, cashiers, ticket clerks, postal workers, and administrative assistants. These are jobs defined by repetitive, rule-based tasks — exactly what AI does best.

In finance, entry-level analyst work — pulling data, drafting initial memos, reconciling reports — is increasingly being handled by AI copilot tools. Goldman Sachs estimates that business and financial operations sit at about 35% task automation already. That number is only going up.

In tech, the irony is sharp: the industry that built AI is now being disrupted by it. Software engineers, product managers, and data analysts are all feeling pressure as AI handles more of the work that used to require junior-to-mid-level human effort.

Geoffrey Hinton's Warning

Geoffrey Hinton — the man often called the "Godfather of AI" — left Google in 2023 specifically so he could speak freely about AI's risks. His warning has aged into a prediction that's coming true.

Hinton has said the pace of AI progress has exceeded even his own expectations, and that we're now in territory that threatens a broad range of white-collar roles. He compared the current moment to the Industrial Revolution — which didn't just change jobs, it eliminated entire categories of human labour that never came back in the same form.

"Tasks that once took AI systems a minute to complete have expanded to projects lasting an hour. Within a few years, AI will be capable of managing work that spans months." — Geoffrey Hinton

But Wait — Isn't AI Also Creating Jobs?

Yes. And this part matters too. AI is creating new roles — prompt engineers, AI trainers, model evaluators, AI ethics officers, automation consultants. Companies investing in AI need humans to manage, fine-tune, and deploy these systems.

The problem isn't that AI creates no jobs. The problem is the speed mismatch. Jobs are being eliminated faster than new ones are being created, and the new jobs require skills that the workers being displaced often don't have — yet.

A 45-year-old bank teller doesn't automatically become an AI systems manager. That transition takes time, money, and training — and most companies aren't providing it.

What This Means If You're Starting Out Right Now

If you're in college, just graduated, or early in your career, this is the most important thing to understand: the entry-level jobs that used to be the training ground for careers are disappearing. The traditional path — get a junior role, learn on the job, work your way up — is getting harder to walk.

What actually protects you right now:

  • Learn to use AI tools, not compete with them. The people who will thrive are those who use AI to multiply their output, not those who ignore it.
  • Develop skills AI can't replicate easily — creative thinking, human judgment, relationship management, storytelling, leadership.
  • Specialise deeply. Generalist roles are the most exposed. Deep expertise in a niche is much harder to automate.
  • Build in public. A blog, a portfolio, a social presence — something that shows what you can do beyond a resume. Exactly what you're reading right now is an example of that.

The Bottom Line

The question "Will AI take over jobs?" has been answered. A better question is: which jobs, how fast, and what do you do about it?

The disruption is real. It's hitting Gen Z hardest. It's accelerating, not slowing down. And the companies doing it aren't apologising — they're calling it efficiency.

The people who come out ahead won't be the ones who were scared of AI. They'll be the ones who understood it, used it, and stayed irreplaceable anyway.
