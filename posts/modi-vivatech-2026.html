Modi Just Told the World: "For India, AI Means All-Inclusive" — Here's What He Pitched at VivaTech 2026

By Yash June 21, 2026 5 min read

Standing in Paris alongside French President Emmanuel Macron at VivaTech 2026 — Europe's largest technology and startup event — Prime Minister Narendra Modi didn't just talk about India's AI ambitions. He made a direct pitch to the world's biggest tech leaders and investors: come build with us.

India served as the AI Country Partner for this year's 10th edition of VivaTech, and Modi used the platform to lay out a vision that was equal parts philosophy and business pitch. His core message, repeated throughout the address: "For India, AI means 'All Inclusive.'"

💬 Modi's words: "Technology can lead to progress only when it is democratized. India believes that in this era of disruption, technology must deliver for all."

The Pitch: Government Enables, Industry Innovates

Modi laid out India's role with a clean, quotable framework: "Our government will enable and industry will innovate. Startups will disrupt and global partners will scale with us." It's a deliberate positioning — India isn't trying to be the company building the next frontier AI model. It's trying to be the platform where everyone else builds theirs, backed by scale, talent, and government support.

To back that up, Modi highlighted that India's "open society, vast talent pool, business-friendly reforms and targeted incentives" amount to over $50 billion in opportunities now available to global investors and innovators looking to scale AI solutions in or with India.

The Numbers Modi Used to Make His Case

Modi didn't just talk in abstractions — he backed the pitch with India's existing digital infrastructure track record:

  • 200,000+ startups — one of the world's largest and fastest-growing innovation ecosystems
  • UPI now accounts for roughly half of the world's real-time digital transactions, and is even usable in France at the Eiffel Tower and Paris airports
  • DigiLocker — one of the world's largest digital document wallets, used by 700 million people
  • The world's largest digital identity system and one of the largest digital payment platforms

The argument: if India can build and scale digital public infrastructure at this level for payments and identity, it can do the same for AI — and any company partnering with India gets to build on top of that foundation rather than starting from scratch.

Real Example, Not Just Talk: Meet Sarlaben

To make the "all-inclusive" framing concrete rather than just a slogan, Modi cited Sarlaben — a voice-first AI assistant launched by Amul that helps women dairy farmers manage livestock nutrition and health. It's a deliberately chosen example: not a flashy chatbot or coding assistant, but AI solving a real, unglamorous problem for people who are usually left out of the tech conversation entirely.

That's the throughline of Modi's pitch — AI's value isn't proven by how impressive the model is, but by how many people it actually reaches and helps.

The India Pavilion: 80+ Startups on Display

Modi personally invited global investors and tech leaders to visit the India Pavilion at VivaTech, which featured more than 80 Indian deep-tech companies and startups. The showcase ranged from the world's first single-piece 3D-printed rocket engine to advanced genetic-engineering therapies, AI systems that detect cancer, cybersecurity tools, autonomous robotics, and smart city and mobility solutions.

Why Paris, Why Now

The timing wasn't accidental. 2026 marks the conclusion of the India-European Union Free Trade Agreement, and the launch of the India-France Year of Innovation — both of which Modi referenced directly, framing this VivaTech appearance as a continuation of deepening India-Europe technology ties rather than a standalone moment. He also drew a deliberate parallel to his last VivaTech address in 2021, made during the depths of COVID-19, framing innovation as the constant thread connecting both moments of global disruption.

What This Means Beyond the Speech

Speeches like this matter less for what's said and more for what follows. Modi's "all-inclusive AI" framing is a deliberate alternative to how the US and China often talk about AI — as a race for dominance. India is instead positioning itself as the place where global AI capital and capability come to scale responsibly and inclusively, leveraging its enormous population, startup base, and digital public infrastructure as the pitch.

Whether that framing converts into real capital inflows, AI infrastructure investment, and job creation on the ground is the part that will actually matter for India's youth and startups in the coming months — not the applause in the room in Paris.

🔥 Bottom line: Modi's closing line to the room said it all: "Work with India and deliver for all." It's a confident, deliberately inclusive pitch — and one that puts India firmly in the global conversation about who gets to shape how AI actually reaches people, not just how powerful it becomes.
