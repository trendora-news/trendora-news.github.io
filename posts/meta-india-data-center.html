Meta Just Picked India as Its Next AI Power Base — Here's Why Jamnagar

By Yash June 19, 2026 5 min read

While the world's biggest tech companies fight over GPUs and compute deals worth billions, Meta just made a quieter but equally important move — and it's putting India right at the center of it. According to a report from Moneycontrol, Meta is planning to lease its first AI-enabled data center in Jamnagar, India.

It might sound like just another infrastructure announcement. It isn't. This is Meta signaling that India isn't just a market for AI products anymore — it's becoming a place where the AI itself gets built and run.

Why Jamnagar?

Jamnagar isn't a random pick. The Gujarat city is already home to some of India's largest industrial and energy infrastructure, including massive refining and petrochemical complexes. That existing industrial backbone — power supply, land availability, logistics, and connectivity — makes it a natural location for the kind of energy-hungry, large-scale data centers that modern AI workloads demand.

AI data centers aren't like regular server farms. Training and running large AI models requires enormous, consistent power supply and cooling infrastructure. Cities with existing heavy-industry power capacity have a real head start — and Jamnagar fits that profile well.

What "AI-Enabled" Actually Means Here

An AI-enabled data center isn't just a building full of servers — it's built specifically to handle the GPU-dense, power-hungry hardware needed for training and running large AI models, as opposed to traditional data centers built for websites, apps, and storage. Meta leasing one in India means heavier AI compute — the kind used to train and serve models like Meta's own Llama series — could increasingly run on Indian soil.

🏭 Why this matters: Where AI compute physically lives matters. A country hosting AI infrastructure gets jobs, energy investment, and strategic leverage — not just access to AI as a consumer.

India's Bigger Play: Becoming a Chip and Infrastructure Hub

This Meta deal doesn't exist in isolation. It fits into India's broader, deliberate push to become a global hub for both AI infrastructure and semiconductor/chip development. The government has been actively courting global tech giants — through incentive schemes, land allocation, and policy support — to set up manufacturing and data infrastructure on Indian soil rather than just selling products into the Indian market.

Combined with India's existing strengths — a massive engineering talent pool, growing domestic AI demand (as the recent BCG study on India's AI revenue optimism showed), and now sovereign AI players like Sarvam AI — this data center deal adds another layer: India becoming a place where the physical infrastructure of AI is built, not just consumed.

What This Means for Jobs and the Economy

Data centers of this scale don't just need software engineers — they create demand for electrical engineers, cooling and HVAC specialists, construction and facilities management roles, and a wide supply chain of local vendors and contractors. For Gujarat specifically, and India more broadly, this is the kind of infrastructure investment that creates jobs well beyond the usual "AI engineer" roles people associate with the AI boom.

It also strengthens India's negotiating position. The more physical AI infrastructure global companies build in India, the more India becomes a country that AI giants need — not just one they sell to.

The Global Context

This comes at a moment when AI compute access has become deeply geopolitical — the U.S. recently restricted foreign access to Anthropic's most advanced models, and Google is paying SpaceX hundreds of millions a month just to secure enough GPU capacity. In that climate, countries that host their own AI infrastructure — rather than depending entirely on foreign data centers — are in a structurally stronger position. Meta's Jamnagar move is a step toward India having exactly that kind of leverage.

🔥 Bottom line: Meta leasing an AI data center in Jamnagar isn't flashy news — but it's one of those quiet moves that compounds over time. Combined with Sarvam AI's rise and India's AI revenue optimism, the picture is clear: India isn't just adopting AI, it's building the ground floor of it.
